The threat quantum computers pose to encryption is growing faster than many anticipated. Historically, the internet has relied on algorithms like [RSA encryption](https://brilliant.org/wiki/rsa-encryption/) and [Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)](https://blog.cloudflare.com/a-relatively-easy-to-understand-primer-on-elliptic-curve-cryptography/) to exchange encryption keys and verify identities across the web. These algorithms are in danger of being entirely broken by the new capabilities of quantum computers, leaving our communications insecure. Modern breakthroughs in research are bringing us closer to this becoming a reality than we were expecting.

Quantum computers can be thought of as computers, but with unique capabilities. Instead of using bits as memory, they use [qubits](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/qubit). Quantum computers can run specialised "quantum algorithms" which in some circumstances are much faster than conventional algorithms. RSA and ECC seemed secure when we designed them, but as we research more about quantum computers, we realise that they aren't secure at all. To resolve this, we have now designed post-quantum algorithms capable of securing data even after quantum computers are accessible.

Shor's algorithm - the algorithm responsible for factorising the integers used in RSA encryption - has [been improved](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.28627v1) by Oratomic and requires far fewer qubits than before. Quantum algorithms used for breaking ECC have also been made [significantly more efficient](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.28846) by Google. Major tech companies like Google, Microsoft, and IBM are competing to build bigger and faster quantum computers, bringing us closer to these quantum algorithms becoming feasible.

With these developments, the advent of quantum computing is closer than we had once anticipated. In response, Google has announced a [2029 timeline](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/safety-security/cryptography-migration-timeline/) for using post-quantum cryptography, and [Cloudflare has followed suit](https://blog.cloudflare.com/post-quantum-roadmap/) with their global network. It won't be long until other major providers follow.

![Screenshot of Cloudflare Radar showing cisa.gov not supporting post-quantum cryptography](./cisa-post-quantum.png)

In the course of researching this, I discovered that America's Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency surprisingly still doesn't support post-quantum cryptography. If you want to check some websites yourself, [Cloudflare offers a free tool](https://radar.cloudflare.com/post-quantum) where you can enter any URL and check.

Adversaries don't have quantum computers yet, with experts in the field predicting that quantum computers capable of breaking RSA are [likely to be built in 10-15 years.](https://globalriskinstitute.org/publication/quantum-threat-timeline-report-2025b/) However, that doesn't mean we should be complacent.

Malicious groups are already harvesting huge quantities of our encrypted communications - known as [Harvest Now, Decrypt Later](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025093pap.pdf) (HNDL) attacks - knowing they may be able to decrypt them later. The bright side is that many of our communications will no longer be valuable after 10 years. Personal IDs like passports expire, and credit card details change. The threat is more prevalent for businesses and governments, where trade secrets and foreign intelligence retain value long after they are produced.

In summary, the threat of quantum computers is most critical for businesses and governments, where secrecy of information is paramount. But consumers are still under threat, as HNDL attacks capture vast amounts of personal data, and not all data expires. The transition needs to happen before quantum hardware catches up. That window is narrowing.
